# Universal Humanization Rules

These rules apply to ALL brands regardless of category, positioning, or audience. They eliminate the patterns that make AI-generated copy detectable. Brand-specific additions layer on top of these universals.

---

## Rule 1: Forbidden AI Vocabulary

LLMs overuse certain words that immediately signal bot-written copy. These are NEVER acceptable:

| Category | Forbidden Words |
|----------|----------------|
| Overused Verbs | delve into, leverage, unlock (except literal), unleash, elevate, embark, dive deep, transform (when vague), navigate (except literal), harness |
| Hype Adjectives | cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, game-changing, revolutionary, seamless, robust, comprehensive, holistic, unparalleled, groundbreaking, world-class, best-in-class |
| Corporate Buzzwords | paradigm shift, synergy, disrupt/disruptive, empower, ecosystem (unless literal), solution (say "answer," "tool," or specific product), optimize (overused), leverage |
| Filler Phrases | in today's world, in today's rapidly evolving, it's important to note, arguably, needless to say, at the end of the day, it goes without saying |
| Pretentious Words | tapestry, realm, multifaceted, pinnacle, myriad, plethora, utilize (say "use"), whilst (say "while"), endeavor |
| Vague Promises | experience (as verb), journey (unless literal), curated, bespoke (unless truly custom), crafted (overused in premium) |

**Action:** If a word is on this list, delete it and rewrite from scratch. Don't substitute a synonym from the same register.

## Rule 2: Burstiness (Sentence Length Variation)

AI writing has uniform sentence lengths. Human writing swings dramatically.

**The rule:** In every 10 sentences, include at least 3 short sentences (under 8 words) and at least 2 long sentences (over 20 words). The rest can be medium.

**Example of good burstiness:**

"Most sleep supplements don't work. Not because the ingredients are bad. Because your body destroys them before they reach your bloodstream. Oral supplements pass through your stomach acid, get metabolized by your liver, and lose up to 85% of their active compounds before doing anything useful. Patches bypass all of that. The ingredients go through your skin directly into your bloodstream. No stomach. No liver. No waste. It's not complicated. It's just biology that pill companies don't want you to think about."

Short: "Patches bypass all of that." (4 words)
Long: "Oral supplements pass through your stomach acid, get metabolized by your liver, and lose up to 85% of their active compounds before doing anything useful." (25 words)

**What BAD burstiness looks like:**
"Sleep supplements often fail to deliver results. This happens because oral ingestion reduces bioavailability. The digestive system breaks down active ingredients. Liver metabolism further reduces effectiveness. Transdermal patches offer an alternative approach. They deliver ingredients through the skin. This bypasses the digestive system entirely."

Every sentence is 6-10 words. Monotonous. Detectable.

## Rule 3: Mandatory Contractions

Formal writing avoids contractions. Humans use them constantly.

**The rule:** Use contractions for at least 30% of eligible opportunities. "It is" becomes "it's," "do not" becomes "don't," "they are" becomes "they're."

**Exceptions:** Keep formal for emphasis. "This is not a supplement. This is science." The lack of contraction creates emphasis.

**Pattern:** Mix contracted and uncontracted forms. Never go 5+ sentences without a contraction. Never contract every single opportunity either.

---

## Rule 4: Conjunction Starts

Starting sentences with "And," "But," "Or," "So" is conversational and human. LLMs avoid it unless instructed.

**The rule:** Start 10-15% of sentences with a conjunction.

"The ingredients are science-backed. But that's not why people buy. They buy because patches feel different. And different is what they need after years of pills that didn't work."

---

## Rule 5: Strategic Imperfection

Perfect grammar, perfect structure, perfect flow = AI. Humans are messier.

**Techniques:**

**Fragments for emphasis:**
"Backed by science. Tested on real people. No gimmicks."

**Parenthetical asides:**
"The patch delivers compounds through your skin (which sounds simple, but the chemistry behind it is anything but)."

**Self-corrections:**
"It works in about 30 minutes. Actually, most users report feeling it within 20."

**Rhetorical questions:**
"So why hasn't anyone done this before? They have. They just didn't do it well."

**One-word sentences:**
"Finally."
"Exactly."
"Different."

---

## Rule 6: Eliminate Hedging

Hedging is the #1 tell for uncertainty-trained LLMs. Humans making claims are direct.

**Forbidden hedging language:**
- "tends to," "arguably," "might," "possibly," "perhaps"
- "it could be said that," "some may find that"
- "in many cases," "often considered"
- "one of the best" (say "the best" or name what makes it good)

**Replace with direct assertions:**

BAD: "This product might help improve your sleep quality."
GOOD: "This product improves your sleep quality."

BAD: "Many customers tend to report better results."
GOOD: "Customers report better results."

BAD: "It's arguably one of the best options available."
GOOD: "It's the best option if you've tried pills and they didn't work."

---

## Rule 7: Em Dash Prohibition

The em dash (—) is the single most common AI writing tell. Zero tolerance.

**The rule:** NEVER use — in any content. Search for it before finalizing. If found, rewrite.

**Alternatives:**
- Use a period and start a new sentence
- Use a colon for emphasis
- Use parentheses for asides
- Use a comma if the aside is short

BAD: "The patch delivers compounds — including melatonin and valerian — directly through your skin."
GOOD: "The patch delivers compounds (including melatonin and valerian) directly through your skin."
ALSO GOOD: "The patch delivers compounds directly through your skin: melatonin, valerian, and five more."

---

## Rule 8: No Formulaic Patterns

Certain openings and closings are so overused by AI that they're instantly recognizable.

**Forbidden openings:**
- "In today's world..."
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "Imagine a world where..."
- "What if I told you..."
- "Welcome to [brand]..."
- "We're excited to announce..."
- "Picture this..."
- "Let's face it..."

**Forbidden closings:**
- "Experience the difference today."
- "Join the revolution."
- "Don't wait. Order now!"
- "Your journey starts here."
- "Ready to transform your life?"
- "Don't miss out!"
- "The future of [category] is here."

**What to do instead:** Start with a specific fact, a provocative claim, or a customer's pain point. End with a concrete next step, a specific benefit, or a direct CTA that matches the brand voice.

---

## Rule 9: "You" Over "We"

AI defaults to brand-centric language. Humans care about themselves.

**The rule:** Maintain a 2:1 ratio of "you/your" to "we/our" across any piece of content.

**Action:** After writing, count instances. If "we" dominates, reframe sentences from the customer's perspective.

BAD: "We developed a formula that delivers ingredients through the skin. Our technology ensures maximum absorption. We believe in science-backed solutions."

GOOD: "Your body absorbs ingredients directly through the skin. No pills. No waste. You get the full dose, not the 15% that survives your digestive system."

---

## Rule 10: Show, Don't Tell

AI tells. Humans show through examples and scenarios.

**The rule:** For every abstract claim, include a concrete scenario or specific detail.

BAD: "Our patches provide better sleep."
GOOD: "You put the patch on at 10pm. By 10:30, the melatonin is in your bloodstream. By 11, you're asleep. No pills dissolving in your stomach. No 3am wake-up wondering if you took enough."

## Brand-Specific Additions

Beyond these 10 universal rules, each brand's guide should add:

1. **Brand-specific forbidden words** — drawn from avatar research "language to avoid" sections
2. **Brand-specific approved vocabulary** — drawn from avatar "language that resonates" sections
3. **Category-specific claim rules** — from regulatory findings in business validation
4. **Positioning-specific tone rules** — premium brands restrict casual language; budget brands restrict pretentious language
5. **Geography-specific language notes** — UK English vs US English, regional colloquialisms
