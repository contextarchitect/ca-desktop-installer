# Funnel Type Selection & Traffic Alignment

## The Full-Picture Principle

Nothing works in isolation. The ad, the headline, the landing page, the close — they form one continuous experience. Before selecting a funnel type, you must understand:

1. **Where is the audience on the awareness ladder?** (Problem Aware → Solution Aware → Product Aware → Most Aware)
2. **What ad format is driving traffic?** (This determines what the reader already knows and feels)
3. **What emotional state did the ad leave them in?** (This determines whether the funnel should lead with logic or emotion)

Every decision flows from these three questions.

## Step 1: Ad-to-Page Alignment (MANDATORY)

Before selecting funnel type or writing any content, identify the traffic source. The ad determines the reader's mental state on arrival, which determines what the funnel page must do.

### Traffic Source → Reader State → Page Requirements

| Ad Type | Reader State on Arrival | Headline Must... | Page Must... | Recommended Funnel |
|---------|------------------------|-------------------|---------------|--------------------|
| Ugly/clickbait image (no context) | Curious, unfiltered, no product awareness | Filter to avatar + create relevance | Full emotional journey: story → root cause → mechanism → product | Advertorial (full 9-section) |
| UGC / day-in-the-life | Curious about product, low emotional investment | Lean into emotional weight, no need to re-filter | Can abbreviate problem section; focus on root cause → mechanism → product | Advertorial or Listicle (emotion variant) |
| Long-form VSL / mini-VSL | Emotionally invested, may be product-curious | Match VSL tone, don't repeat the emotional arc | Focus on logic: root cause + mechanism + product validation | Listicle (logic variant) |
| Targeted image + headline copy | Filtered to avatar, problem-aware | Go deeper into specifics, can skip broad filtering | Can start at root cause or mechanism | Advertorial (abbreviated) or Listicle |
| Advertorial as ad copy (long primary text) | Emotionally warmed, product-curious | Bridge to logical validation | Logic-focused reinforcement of what the ad established | Listicle (logic variant) — COMBO PATTERN |
| Quiz / interactive | Self-identified, high intent | Acknowledge their quiz results, feel personalized | Personalized solution positioning | Advertorial (product-aware variant) |

### Filtering Rules

If the ad does NOT filter the audience to the specific avatar, the funnel page MUST do the filtering. This happens through:

1. **Headline** — mentions the specific condition, demographic, or situation
2. **Subheadline** — reinforces specificity
3. **Above-the-fold image** — shows someone/something that matches the avatar

If the ad ALREADY filters (mentions the condition, targets precisely, uses avatar-specific language), the funnel page can skip filtering and go straight to emotional depth or logical validation.

## Step 2: Funnel Type Selection Matrix

### By Awareness Stage and Price Point

| Awareness Stage | Low-Ticket | Mid-Ticket | High-Ticket |
|----------------|------------|------------|-------------|
| Problem Aware | Listicle (education) or Advertorial (story) | Advertorial (full 9-section) | Advertorial (deep trust + full journey) |
| Solution Aware | Listicle (logic or product variant) | Either (based on ad type) | Advertorial (extensive mechanism + buildup) |
| Product Aware | Listicle (product variant, short) | Advertorial (social proof + close heavy) | Advertorial (case study + premium positioning) |
| Most Aware | Direct to product page | Direct to product page | Direct to product page |

### Decision Signals

**Choose ADVERTORIAL when:**
- Avatar has tried solutions before and failed (skepticism high → need "nothing worked" loop)
- Product requires emotional connection beyond features
- Root cause explanation is complex or counterintuitive
- The unique mechanism needs narrative context to be believed
- Story naturally bridges problem to solution
- Traffic comes from low-context ads (ugly images, clickbait)
- Higher price point requires extensive perceived value building

**Choose LISTICLE when:**
- Traffic source already created emotional investment (VSL, advertorial ad copy, emotional UGC)
- Educational content naturally fits numbered format
- Multiple factors contribute to the problem
- Quick scanning and decision-making expected
- Audience is solution-aware and comparing options
- The ad already warmed them — now they need logical validation

**Choose COMBO PATTERN when:**
- Running ugly/clickbait image ads with long advertorial ad copy
- Audience needs full warming but advertorial happens in the ad, not the landing page
- Want to separate emotional investment (ad) from logical validation (landing page)

## Step 3: Combo Funnel Pattern

The combo pattern uses an advertorial as ad copy paired with a listicle as the landing page.

```
UGLY IMAGE AD (grabs attention, no product context)
  → Long Facebook ad copy IS the advertorial (emotional story, root cause, mechanism teaser)
    → Reader clicks through, emotionally invested
      → LISTICLE landing page (logic-focused: "7 Reasons Women Choose [Product]")
        → Each reason = logical mechanism, ingredient, or proof point
          → CTA → Product page
```

**Key rule:** If the ad copy already created emotional investment, the listicle should be logic-focused. Never double up on emotion — the reader will feel manipulated.

## Awareness Stage Content Guidance

### Problem Aware

The avatar knows they have a problem but hasn't researched solutions.

**MC Principle:** The majority of the advertorial is the emotional journey and root cause explanation. The product appears late and briefly. You are educating and connecting before you are selling.

**Tone balance:** Emotion-first, logic-second. The story, pain identification, and root cause explanation dominate. Mechanism and product sections are present but concise. Most of the reader's time is spent feeling understood and finally learning why nothing has worked.

**CTA approach:** Predominantly soft CTAs; hard CTA only after product reveal. Don't ask them to buy before they understand why they should.

**Social proof type:** General validation, volume metrics, relatable testimonials.

### Solution Aware

The avatar knows solutions exist but doesn't know which is best or that YOUR solution exists.

**MC Principle:** The problem is acknowledged briefly — they already know it. The focus shifts to root cause differentiation and mechanism explanation. Position your approach as the one that actually addresses the real issue, unlike everything else they've tried.

**Tone balance:** Balanced between emotion and logic. Emotional connection is still present (especially in the "nothing worked" loop) but the mechanism and product sections carry more weight. Show why THIS approach is different.

**CTA approach:** Mixed soft and hard; multiple CTAs throughout.

**Social proof type:** Category success stories, your brand testimonials, comparison data.

### Product Aware

The avatar knows your product but isn't convinced to buy.

**MC Principle:** Minimal problem/mechanism re-education. The reader already understands. Focus on objection handling, social proof, close architecture, and making the purchase feel safe, easy, and obvious.

**Tone balance:** Logic-forward with emotional reinforcement. The product and close sections dominate. Features, proof, guarantee, and price architecture do the heavy lifting.

**CTA approach:** Hard CTAs dominant; emphasis on risk reversal and urgency.

**Social proof type:** Brand testimonials, volume, recency, specific outcomes.

## Visual Break Principle

**MC Principle:** "Scroll a bit, image, scroll a bit, image." Walls of text make people bounce. Visual breaks keep them scrolling. Even if the content is long, it should never FEEL long.

**Practical proxy:** No more than 3-4 short paragraphs of body text between visual elements (images, infographics, diagrams, social proof screenshots, CTA cards). Each paragraph should be 2-3 sentences. If you count four consecutive paragraphs without a visual break, insert one.

This applies to both advertorials and listicles, though listicles naturally enforce it through the image-per-item structure.

## Visual Density Guidance by Price Point

**MC Principle:** Simpler products with lower price points need less text justification — images and social proof carry the weight. Complex or expensive products need more detailed explanation and mechanism coverage.

| Price Point | Visual Density | What This Means |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Low-ticket | Image-heavy, text-light | Lean on images, social proof visuals, short punchy paragraphs. Let the visuals do most of the persuasion. |
| Mid-ticket | Balanced | Images and text carry equal weight. Root cause and mechanism need explanation, but keep it visually supported. |
| High-ticket | Text can be denser, but still visually supported | Detailed mechanism, product buildup, and close sections. But still break every 3-4 paragraphs with visuals. |

## Image Authenticity Rules

**MC Principle:** Good listicles and advertorials use realistic, iPhone-quality images showing real people — not studio photography or AI-generated perfection. People smiling triggers dopamine. Normal body types create identification. Aspirational models alienate struggling audiences.

| Context | Image Style | Why |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Problem/story sections | iPhone-quality, real people, no studio | Authenticity builds trust; polished = advertising |
| Root cause / mechanism | Clean infographics, diagrams | Clarity aids System 1 understanding |
| Social proof / testimonials | iPhone selfies, real user photos | Relatable people trigger identification |
| Product showcase (CTA cards) | Polished product photography | Product should look premium and real |
| Solution / transformation | Mix of real + aspirational | Show real results, not stock photo perfection |

**Rules:**
- Show people who look like the avatar (same demographics, body type, life stage)
- Smiling faces in solution/positive contexts (dopamine trigger)
- Never use aspirational models to sell to struggling audiences (creates shame, not motivation)
- Product images can be polished; people images should feel authentic
- Social proof images should look like real user-generated content

## Universal Conversion Principles

These apply to ALL funnel types:

1. **First-person CTA copy:** "Get my [product]" outperforms "Get your [product]" by 90%
2. **Social proof under CTAs:** Increases conversion 68%
3. **Trust signals above fold:** Increases conversion 42% for unknown brands
4. **Odd-numbered lists:** 20% higher CTR than even (7 is psychologically optimal)
5. **Negative superlatives:** "Worst" outperforms "Best" by 63% in headlines
6. **Sticky mobile CTA:** Up to 253% increase in order completion
7. **Centered CTAs:** 682% more clicks than left-aligned
8. **Page load under 3 seconds:** Every 0.1s improvement = 8.4% conversion increase
9. **Video on landing pages:** Up to 80% higher conversion (but 60% drop-off after 2 min)
10. **Personalized CTAs:** 202% better performance than generic

## Scroll Depth Optimization

Users scrolling 75%+ are 2x more likely to convert. These techniques increase scroll depth:

1. **Avoid full-width horizontal lines** — creates "false floor" perception
2. **Cut visual elements at fold line** — image partially visible below fold signals continuation
3. **Maintain content density** — excessive white space signals "nothing more to see"
4. **Use sub-headlines visible while scanning** — gives preview of what's below
5. **Strategic CTA placement** — don't front-load hard CTAs; pull readers deeper before selling
6. **No "In this article" openings** — wastes prime above-fold real estate
7. **Content cut at mobile fold** — ensure something is visually cut off to signal scrolling

**Above-the-fold requirements:**
- Headline fully visible
- Opening text started (at least 50% visible on mobile)
- Visual element cut at fold line (signals continuation)
- Trust signal if brand is unknown
- CTA should NOT appear above fold on cold traffic (feels premature)
